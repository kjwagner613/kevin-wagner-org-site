Overview

Domestic Violence Classification (Threshold Issue)

Both charges depend on proving the complainant is a "family or household member" under ORS 135.230.

From the case review, there was:

  • No romantic or sexual relationship
  • No shared finances or responsibilities
  • Separate beds
  • Temporary guest status
  • She is engaged to someone else
  • No shared child

Under Oregon law, "cohabitation" requires a marriage-like relationship, not simply living under the same roof.

If that element fails, the DV designation fails, and Count 1 may lose felony enhancement.

From the identified evidence gaps:

  • No photos
  • No medical exam
  • No visible injuries documented

This is significant in a strangulation case, where officers are trained to document injuries and encourage medical evaluation.

Motions to Consider

  • Motion to dismiss DV enhancement (failure to meet ORS 135.230)
  • Motion to reduce Count 1 (no aggravator means misdemeanor treatment)
  • Motion for judgment of acquittal on Count 2 (failure of statutory elements)
  • Motion to compel missing evidence (bodycam, 911, medical, statements)

Critical Points

  • Felony enhancement may be unsupported
  • DV classification appears questionable
  • Menacing elements may be unmet
  • Strangulation lacks corroboration
  • Complainant was intoxicated and later recanting
  • No medical or photographic evidence

Summary of Issues to Develop

  1. Whether the State can prove a qualifying "family or household member" relationship under ORS 135.230.
  2. Whether Count 1 qualifies as a felony under ORS 163.187(4), and if not, whether it must be reduced.
  3. Whether the elements of menacing (intent, imminence, substantial step) are met.
  4. Whether the DV designation is legally supportable under ORS 132.586.
  5. Whether the evidence supports "impeding breathing" as required for strangulation.
  6. Whether bodycam, 911 audio, medical records, and witness statements contradict the indictment.
  7. Whether motions to dismiss, reduce, or exclude evidence are appropriate.

Current position: the case appears to lack the evidentiary foundation required to sustain the charges as written.

Reasoning

1. Indictment Theory of Domestic Violence Status

Key issue: Whether the State can legally classify the alleged conduct as domestic violence under Oregon law.

Both counts depend on proving a qualifying relationship under ORS 135.230.

Counsel should challenge whether the State can prove cohabitation factors, not just shared roof space.

  • No shared finances
  • No romantic or sexual relationship
  • No shared responsibilities
  • Separate beds
  • Temporary guest status
  • She has a fiance elsewhere

2. Count 1: Strangulation (Class C Felony)

Key issue: Whether any statutory aggravator exists.

The indictment appears to rely on the "family or household member" aggravator. If that fails, Count 1 may reduce from Class C felony to Class A misdemeanor.

Counsel should closely review whether the State can prove "impeding breathing" without medical evidence, photos, or objective observations.

3. Count 2: Menacing (Domestic Violence)

Key issue: Whether intent, imminence, and substantial step can be proven.

When police arrived, the defendant was asleep in bed and she was in another room. This contradiction should be emphasized against claims of imminent threat.

4. Domestic Violence Designation

Key issue: Whether the DV label is legally supportable under the relationship facts.

If the relationship element fails, the DV designation should be removed. That affects sentencing, probation terms, reporting, and future background implications in the case.

5. Evidentiary Weaknesses to Investigate

  • Body-worn camera footage (demeanor, statements, condition)
  • 911 call and dispatch logs
  • Medical records or lack of records
  • Witness statements and changes over time
  • Relationship evidence showing no qualifying DV relationship

6. Procedural Issues

  • Motion to dismiss DV enhancements if the relationship definition fails
  • Motion to reduce Count 1 if no aggravator applies
  • MJOA on menacing if intent, imminence, and substantial step are not proven
  • Motion to compel discovery if key evidence was not produced

7. Narrative Issues

The case narrative should stay clear and consistent, supported by evidence like messages, timeline reconstruction, and separate sleeping setup.

Strategy: Likely Prosecution Moves and Case Counters

1. Hearsay Exceptions

Likely move: Try to admit statements through 911, bodycam, scene statements, or medical channels.

Counter focus: Challenge admissibility, confrontation issues, timing, and reliability.

2. "No marks does not mean no strangulation"

Likely move: General claim that strangulation can occur without visible injury.

Counter focus: General possibility is not proof in this case. The burden remains proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

3. "Victims recant" Narrative

Likely move: Frame recantation as common DV behavior.

Counter focus: Keep the jury on the actual facts, specific reliability issues, and contradictions.

4. Officer-Credibility Substitution

Likely move: Build case around officer testimony instead of live complainant testimony.

Counter focus: Officer did not witness strangulation and cannot replace cross-examination of the accuser.

5. Medical Notes as "Objective" Proof

Likely move: Use vague symptoms or "patient reports" as corroboration.

Counter focus: Distinguish subjective reports from objective findings, and challenge weak or non-specific records.

6. Defendant Statements

Likely move: Reframe any ambiguous statement as an admission.

Counter focus: Pin exact wording, context, and whether elements are truly proven.

7. Emotional Appeal ("Why would she lie?")

Likely move: Ask jurors to infer truth from accusation seriousness.

Counter focus: Bring focus back to evidence quality, reliability, and constitutional burden of proof.

8. Confrontation Clause Workaround Attempts

Likely move: Argue statements are non-testimonial due to emergency context.

Counter focus: If alleged perpetrator was asleep when police arrived and no active emergency existed, statements look investigative and should face confrontation limits.

Closing position: Narrative frameworks cannot replace evidence. Every argument should return to admissibility, corroboration, cross-examination, and proof beyond a reasonable doubt.
