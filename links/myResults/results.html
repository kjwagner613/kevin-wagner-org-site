  2. Whether the elements of menacing (intent, imminence, substantial step) are met.
  3. Whether the DV designation is legally supportable under ORS 132.586.
  4. Whether the evidence supports “impeding breathing” as required for strangulation.

Reasoning

1. Indictment Theory of Domestic Violence Status

Key issue: Whether the State can legally classify the alleged conduct as “domestic violence” under Oregon law.

Both counts depend on proving a qualifying “family or household member” relationship under ORS 135.230.

Requires: “Victim is family member or household member as defined in ORS 135.230.”

Argument: “She was a guest staying for a little while… no shared expenses, responsibilities… two beds here… she is engaged.”

Need to examine: whether the State can prove “cohabitation” under Oregon law.

The statute requires a marriage-like relationship, not simply living under the same roof (“nature of the relationship… shared residence and mutual responsibilities… presentation as a couple”).

Points to argue:

  • No shared finances
  • No romantic or sexual relationship
  • No shared responsibilities
  • Separate beds
  • Temporary guest status
  • She has a fiancé elsewhere

This is a threshold issue. If the relationship does not qualify, both charges lose their DV enhancement, and Count 1 may lose felony status entirely.

2. Count 1: Strangulation (Class C Felony)

Key issue: Whether any statutory aggravator exists.

The felony-elevating factors under ORS 163.187(4): “Victim is family or household member… victim under 10… weapon… prior convictions… pregnancy…”

Attorney should analyze:

A. Which aggravator the State is relying on

The indictment states: “The victim is a family or household member.”

This suggests reliance solely on the “family or household member” aggravator. If that fails, Count 1 may reduce from a Class C felony to a Class A misdemeanor.

B. Whether the State can prove “impeding breathing”

The indictment alleges: “impede the normal breathing or blood circulation… by applying pressure on the throat and neck or chest…”

Items to examine:

  • Medical evidence: none (did not take her to hospital)
  • Photographs: none
  • Officer observations: if injury was observed, medical attention should have been pushed
  • Statements made at the scene
  • Whether alleged conduct is physically consistent with “impeding breathing”

If evidence is weak or inconsistent, this is a major defense point.

3. Count 2: Menacing (Domestic Violence)

Key issue: Whether the statutory elements are met.

Test language: “IF the person acted intentionally… IF the act was intended to place another in fear… IF the threatened harm was imminent…”

Focus areas:

A. Intent

“When the police got here, I was asleep in my bed… no interaction going on.”

State may not be able to prove:

  • Intentional conduct
  • An attempt
  • Imminence
  • A substantial step

This is a major evidentiary contradiction.

B. Imminence

“Imminent” means immediate and impending, not remote or speculative.

If the alleged threat occurred earlier, and police found both parties in separate rooms asleep or calm, imminence is undermined.

C. Substantial Step

Menacing is an attempt crime. Questions:

  • What conduct constitutes the “substantial step”?
  • Is there evidence of action taken at the time police arrived?
  • Is the allegation purely retrospective?

If the only evidence is a statement made after the fact, it is a weak menacing case.

4. Domestic Violence Designation

Key issue: Whether the DV label is legally supportable.

“Domestic violence designation applies when the victim and offender are in a qualifying relationship under ORS 132.586.”

Analyze:

  • Whether the State can prove the relationship
  • Whether the DV designation is improperly applied
  • Whether the indictment’s DV language is conclusory rather than factual

If the relationship fails, the DV designation must be removed.

This affects:

  • Firearm rights
  • Sentencing
  • Probation terms
  • Future background checks
  • Federal reporting

5. Evidentiary Weaknesses to Investigate

A. Body-worn camera footage (verify condition, demeanor, statements, physical evidence)

B. 911 call and dispatch logs (who called, what was said, fear expressed, contradictions)

C. Medical records (signs of strangulation, injuries, inconsistencies)

D. Witness statements (corroboration, statement changes)

E. Prior relationship evidence to establish:

  • No romantic relationship
  • No cohabitation
  • No shared finances
  • No shared responsibilities

This directly attacks DV classification.

6. Procedural Issues

  • Motion to Dismiss DV Enhancements: if relationship definition fails, DV designation is improper.
  • Motion to Reduce Count 1 to Misdemeanor: if no aggravator applies, Count 1 cannot be a felony.
  • Motion for Judgment of Acquittal (MJOA) on Menacing: if intent, imminence, and substantial step are not proven, menacing cannot stand.
  • Motion to Compel Discovery: if medical evidence, bodycam, witness statements, or photos have not been produced.

7. Narrative Issues

“She was a guest… temporary help for a friend… she is engaged… no shared infrastructure.”

Prepare:

  • Clear, consistent narrative
  • Corroborating evidence (texts, messages, lease, photos of separate beds)
  • Timeline reconstruction
  • Evidence of her fiancé and living situation

This is essential for undermining DV classification.

State theory challenge: no physical evidence, no corroboration, and complainant recantation.

Additional Breakdown

1. Legal Elements Require Evidence

The State must prove:

  • Impeding breathing for strangulation
  • Intent + imminence + substantial step for menacing
  • A qualifying domestic relationship for DV status

There were no photographs, no medical attention, no witnesses. She was intoxicated at the time. She was recanting when I saw her in court at the last court date, Jan 30th, 2026.

These are direct contradictions to factual assertions in the indictment.

2. Lack of Medical Evidence

Strangulation cases normally require documented indicators such as:

  • Petechiae
  • Bruising
  • Redness
  • Hoarseness
  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Medical evaluation
  • Photographs
  • Officer observations

Without corroboration, the State’s burden becomes extremely difficult.

3. No Photographs = No Contemporaneous Documentation

Emphasize: “They did not take any photographs.”

This matters because the indictment claims: “impede the normal breathing or blood circulation… by applying pressure on the throat or neck…”

If officers believed that happened, they would normally photograph:

  • The neck
  • The chest
  • The face
  • The scene

The absence of photos is a glaring omission.

4. No Hospital Transport = No Medical Corroboration

They did not take her to the hospital.

In strangulation cases, officers are trained to strongly encourage medical evaluation because internal injuries can be invisible.

If they did not, it suggests:

  • They did not observe signs consistent with strangulation
  • They did not believe medical risk was present
  • They did not see evidence supporting the allegation

This is a major point that should be explored.

5. Recantation Is a Critical Factor

This is something that should be aggressively pursued:

  • Written recantation
  • Recorded recantation
  • Sworn affidavit
  • Text messages
  • Statements to third parties
  • Statements to police after the fact

While recantation does not automatically end a case, it dramatically weakens the State’s position, especially when combined with:

  • No injuries
  • No photos
  • No medical evidence
  • No witnesses
  • No corroboration
  • No DV qualifying relationship

This is the kind of record that creates a structurally unsound case.

7. Being Found Asleep Is Incompatible with “Imminent Threat”

When the police arrived, I was asleep in my bed. She was in bed in the other room.

This directly contradicts the indictment’s claim that I was: “grabbing her by the throat, squeezing, while yelling that I was going to kill her.”

“Imminence cannot exist when the accused is asleep in another room.” This seems a textbook failure of the statutory elements.

8. The DV Relationship Element Is Unsupported

  • No romantic relationship
  • No sexual relationship
  • No shared finances
  • No shared responsibilities
  • Separate beds
  • Temporary guest
  • She has a fiancé elsewhere

Cite ORS 135.230 and the cohabitation factors.

“If the relationship fails, the DV designation fails, and the felony enhancement fails.”

This is a threshold issue and should be challenged immediately.

A. Demand Full Discovery

  • Bodycam
  • 911 call
  • Dispatch logs
  • Officer reports
  • Medical records (if any)
  • Witness statements
  • Any recorded recantations

B. Challenge the DV Classification

Use ORS 135.230 and the documented factors.

C. Challenge the Felony Enhancement

If DV classification fails, Count 1 cannot be a felony.

D. Challenge the Elements of Menacing

  • No intent
  • No imminence
  • No substantial step
  • No confrontation
  • I was asleep

E. Challenge the Elements of Strangulation

  • No medical evidence
  • No photos
  • No officer-observed injuries
  • No corroboration

F. Document the Recantation

This is crucial.

G. Prepare Motions

  • Motion to dismiss DV enhancement
  • Motion to reduce Count 1
  • Motion for judgment of acquittal on Count 2
  • Motion to compel missing evidence

This case appears to lack the evidentiary foundation required to sustain the charges as written. For defense, counsel should probe, aggressively, the absence of corroboration, the recantation, the lack of a qualifying relationship, and the failure to meet statutory elements.
